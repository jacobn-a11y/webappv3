Brand Guide 2026

Inspired by Appfire

A comprehensive design reference capturing the visual DNA of appfire.com — distilled into actionable guidelines for building a brand that channels the same dark, modern, enterprise-premium aesthetic while standing on its own.

Brand DNA 01

Design Principles

The Appfire brand is built on these core visual principles. Your brand should internalize these philosophies, not copy specific executions.

01

Dark-First Confidence

The entire brand leads with a near-black (#090A0F) foundation. This isn't just dark mode — it's the primary identity. White and light sections exist as contrast moments, not the default. This conveys premium, technical confidence.

02

Weightless Typography

Hero headlines are ultra-light (weight 300) at massive scale (up to 96px). This tension between size and delicacy creates a sense of effortless elegance. The type feels like it floats. Body copy stays light too.

03

Restrained Color

Color is used surgically — blue (#336FE6) appears almost exclusively on interactive elements: buttons, links, arrows. The rest of the interface is grayscale. Gradients appear only in hero accent moments using aurora-like teal/blue/purple blends.

04

Generous Whitespace

Sections breathe with 100px+ vertical padding. Content is centered and constrained to ~1200px max-width. There is no visual clutter. The emptiness itself communicates confidence and sophistication.

05

Systematic Navigation

Section labels use a numbered taxonomy ("Solutions — 01", "Trust Center — 03") that gives the site a structured, almost editorial feel. This creates a sense of intentional information architecture.

06

Minimal Decoration

No box shadows. No heavy gradients on cards. Borders are 1px and use muted neutrals. The brand trusts typography, space, and color restraint to do the work. Decoration is reserved for gradient hero moments only.

Color Usage Rules

Do

Use Blue-400 exclusively for buttons, link arrows, and interactive elements. Let color draw the eye to actions.

Don't

Scatter blue throughout headlines, body copy, or decorative elements. Color is a signal, not decoration.

Do

Reserve gradients (teal/blue/purple aurora blends) for large hero moments and feature cards. They should feel special.

Don't

Apply gradients to small UI elements, icons, or text. Gradients lose their impact when overused.

Do

Use weight 300 (light) for all display/hero headings. The contrast between massive size and thin weight is the signature.

Don't

Use bold (700+) weights for headings. Bold headlines destroy the elegant, effortless feel of the brand.

Patterns 06

Signature Motifs

These recurring design patterns define the brand's visual language. They should be adapted — not directly copied — when building your inspired brand.

Inline Photography

The hero section embeds a rounded-rectangle photo within the large headline text. The image appears to be clipped inside a word, creating a striking mixed-media effect where photography and typography merge.

The photo uses a rounded rectangle shape (~8px radius) and sits at roughly 60% opacity or with a subtle overlay so the white text remains readable where it overlaps.

Aurora Gradients

Feature sections and CTA cards use ethereal, multi-stop gradients that evoke a northern lights / aurora effect. The gradient blends deep navy, cobalt blue, teal, and cyan with soft color transitions.

background: linear-gradient( 135deg, #173166 0%, #336FE6 20%, #71CAC1 45%, #A4FDF4 65%, #D1FEF9 85%, #8DFCF1 100% );

Logo Carousel

A scrolling horizontal row of enterprise customer logos in white (monochrome) on the dark background. Auto-scrolling ticker animation. Bordered above and below by thin 1px neutral-300 lines.

Logos are displayed as white SVGs/images for visual consistency. No color logos. The carousel provides social proof without disrupting the minimal aesthetic.

Numbered Sections

Each major page section is tagged with a pill-shaped label containing a section name, a horizontal rule, and a number (01, 02, 03...). This editorial convention gives the page an organized, intentional structure.

Labels are left-aligned within the section and sit above the main heading with 48px of space between them.

Thin Horizontal Dividers

Every major section is separated by a single 1px line in neutral-300 (#2E2D2F). These subtle dividers provide structure without adding visual weight.

Dark Sticky Header

The navigation bar is pinned to the top, uses the same near-black (#090A0F) background, and includes a subtle blue accent line at the bottom edge. The logo sits left; nav links center/right; a search icon is far-right.

Differentiation 09

Making It Yours

To build a brand inspired by Appfire without copying it, shift these key variables while keeping the underlying structure.

Shift the Accent Color

Replace the blue (#336FE6) with your own brand color — a vibrant violet, electric green, or warm amber would all work within the same dark-first framework. The entire system pivots on this single accent.

Choose Your Own Typeface

Area Normal is Appfire's specific typeface. For your brand, select a different geometric sans that has a light (300) weight: General Sans, Satoshi, Cabinet Grotesk, or Switzer are all strong alternatives that maintain the same airy elegance.

Warm or Cool the Neutrals

Appfire's neutrals have a subtle purple/cool undertone. Shift yours warmer (toward brown/taupe) or cooler (toward blue-gray) to create a distinct mood while keeping the same dark-first hierarchy.

Evolve the Gradient Palette

The aurora gradient is a strong signature. Create your own multi-stop gradient using your accent color family — perhaps a sunset (amber → rose → violet) or a forest (emerald → teal → slate) to carve out unique territory.

Add Your Own Motif

Appfire uses inline photography within headlines. Consider your own signature move: animated SVG patterns, a custom illustration style, or kinetic typography to differentiate while keeping the same minimal structural DNA.

Adapt the Logo Language

Appfire's logo is a simple geometric triangle/flame mark + lowercase wordmark. Your mark should be similarly minimal, monoline-friendly (it needs to work in white on dark), and pair with a lowercase or mixed-case wordmark in your chosen typeface.
